# Leadership Team -- Do's and Don'ts

You're the people with the power to invest, allocate, and decide. That makes you the difference between prototypes that become products and prototypes that become resentment. This page exists so you know exactly what's expected of you in this operating model.

Your role is not to run the program. The organising team does that. Your role is to show up with genuine attention, make honest decisions, and back them with actual resources.

---

## Do's

### 1. Take time to learn what people are actually solving

Read the submissions. Watch the demos. Understand the problems people are choosing to spend their own energy on. These choices tell you more about real org pain points than any strategy deck.

### 2. Show up to demo days

Not "send a delegate." You. In the room. Paying attention. People built these prototypes on their own time and initiative. The least leadership can do is be present when they present. Your absence sends a louder message than your feedback ever will.

### 3. Fund what works

When a prototype proves its value and the review panel selects it for investment, actually allocate the resources. That means real engineering time, not "work on it when you have bandwidth." Bandwidth is never free. Allocated time is.

### 4. Give honest, specific feedback on rejections

Every rejected submission deserves to know why. Not a form letter. Not silence. A clear, specific reason: "The business case wasn't strong enough because X" or "This overlaps with an existing initiative Y." Honest rejection builds trust. Silence builds cynicism.

### 5. Protect allocated time

When someone's prototype gets selected for investment, that person needs dedicated time to build it. Protect that time from being clawed back by BAU priorities. If you approve the investment, defend it.

### 6. Recognise submissions from outside engineering

The best prototypes might come from operations, customer service, finance, or HR. These people see problems engineers never encounter. When a non-technical person submits a working prototype, that deserves extra attention -- they had to work harder to build it.

### 7. Use the data to inform strategy

The pattern of what people choose to prototype is a signal. If 30% of submissions are about internal knowledge search, that's your org telling you something. Feed this data back into product strategy and roadmap decisions.

---

## Don'ts

### 1. Don't treat this as a PR exercise

This is not a "look how innovative we are" campaign. If you're launching this to write a LinkedIn post or fill a slide in the annual report, stop. People will see through it in one cycle, and you'll never get genuine submissions again.

### 2. Don't promise funding without follow-through

Nothing kills a prototyping culture faster than "we'll invest in the top ideas" followed by silence. If you commit to funding the top 5 and investing in the top 15, honour that commitment. Every cycle. If budget changes, say so upfront before submissions open.

### 3. Don't pick winners based on politics

The review panel exists for a reason. The rubric exists for a reason. If the VP's pet project scores a 12 and an unknown engineer's submission scores a 22, the engineer wins. Full stop. The moment political selection overrides rubric scoring, the program is dead.

### 4. Don't ignore submissions from non-engineering teams

A prototype from someone in procurement who automated their own workflow with approved AI tools is just as valid as one from a senior engineer. If your selection consistently skews toward engineering-only submissions, something is broken in your evaluation process.

### 5. Don't micromanage prototypes during the investment phase

You selected it. You funded it. Now let the person build it. Check-ins are fine -- the organising team handles those. But don't redesign their solution, redirect their approach mid-build, or load them with requirements they never signed up for. That's how you turn investment into interference.

### 6. Don't let graduated prototypes die in integration limbo

Graduation means a prototype enters the product roadmap. It needs an owner, a team, a timeline. If graduated prototypes consistently stall because no product team wants to adopt them, fix the handoff process. A graduation that leads nowhere is worse than a rejection.

### 7. Don't run this like a hackathon

This is not a one-day event. There are no trophies. There's no pizza-fueled all-nighter. This is a permanent operating model with rolling submissions and quarterly evaluation. If you treat it like an event, you'll get event-quality output: flashy demos with no lasting value.
